Educated, but Unrepresented

American women now earn more bachelors and masters degrees than men. Yet, they hold only 28% of congressional seats. Consider this paradox to reveal a hidden truth: that education alone does not guarantee political power in our country.

60%

Share of bachelor's and master's degrees now earned by women in the US—raising the stakes for the representation gap.

28%

Current share of women in Congress, demonstrating that educational gains still lag in translating to seats.

Our central questions

  • Why does the United States lag behind other wealthy democracies in terms of female political representation, in spite of having one of the highest female educational attainment rates and shares among developed nations?
  • Does women's power contribute to economic growth in America, as it does in other countries?
  • What institutional factors and cultural factors could explain the gap that has been seen in the United States?

Many scholars have investigated how supply (educated women) changes to demand (becoming politicians). Their research shows that academic records alone are not enough. The ability to change who has the educational background to run for office in this country varies based on psychological, acquaintance, and regulatory factors. Survey experiments show significant differences between men and women who say they want to become politicians, even when they are the same intelligence level or have the same academic records [18]. Think about it: the way political parties select candidates shows that who you know and who you can help (not just money) are the reasons they choose you [22]. Furthermore, qualitative and quantitative studies on party gatekeeping show that the way they select and nominate candidates is always based on old-fashioned methods that make it difficult for female candidates. This hinders the demand side. Even when many women have excellent academic records, the hinderance persists [19]. There is also research complementary workplace-network studies and program evaluations which have shown that they are a game changer and important tool to improve recruitment outcomes and can be used for positive change [21].

Why higher education does not guarantee political power

Since the 1980s, American women have attained higher education and are now more likely to earn bachelor's and master's degrees than any other country. Claudia Goldin, an economist at Harvard University specializing in gender and economic history, calls this a "reversal of the college attainment gap" [1]. Yet, women hold only 28% of congressional seats, a phenomenon that, though improved over time, still persists even after 50 years of feminist activism. Research on political ambition and recruitment helps explain why higher credentials do not automatically become candidacies: Lawless & Fox document gendered gaps in political ambition and interviewer effects that depress women's expressed willingness to run [18]. Longitudinal studies also document occupational transitions, particularly out of STEM into education and health, which reshape the professional pipelines available to many female graduates and reduce visibility in networks that lead to nominations [23].

This issue upends a broader American perspective: people believe that education increases political power. Yet, according to research by Cameron Arnzen and Sarah Cohodes, economists studying educational equity and political participation, younger women vote at much higher rates than men; in our analysis of matched education and voter records, young women vote at a rate roughly 5.8 times higher than young men [12]. Still, this turnout does not automatically translate into leadership power.

To explore why academic achievement does not translate into political power, this project leverages World Bank gender data to compare 170 countries from 1970 to the present ([D1]). Through our analysis of these data we find that the U.S. remains at about 28% legislative representation while other wealthy democracies perform better: Canada ~34%, Germany ~35%, and the Nordic countries ~40–47% (World Bank). As such, we frame three questions central to the project: why the US lags, whether women's power contributes to economic growth in the US as it does elsewhere, and whether institutional or cultural factors best explain the gap.

First, researchers such as Claudia Goldin and Pamela Paxton, Sheri Kunovich, and Melanie Hughes have shown that women now enjoy greater educational attainment in wealthy countries [1]. However, higher education does not necessarily translate into equal political power: Paxton emphasizes a dual framework of supply-side (education, socioeconomic status, and willingness to run) and demand-side (institutional and cultural support) drivers.

Second, scholars debate whether cultural attitudes or institutional rules matter most. Profeta & Woodhouse's comparative work uses Italy's 1993 electoral reform as evidence that electoral rules can sharply change representation even when candidate qualifications are unchanged [2]. Longitudinal work by Sirianne Dahlum and colleagues shows a positive association between women's political empowerment and later GDP per‑capita growth [3], while cross‑national panels such as Jayasuriya & Burke report similar links between female legislative share and later economic performance [4]. Park & Liang document the feedback loop: female legislators improve education and social outcomes that cultivate future female leaders [6].

Finally, research on democratic maturity argues that in less‑developed countries, institutional fixes like quota systems or PR can produce rapid gains; in mature democracies cultural attitudes often matter more. See Tremblay [8] for a comparative perspective. In short, the U.S. appears to be an outlier not because women lack education or ambition, but because specific institutional and cultural barriers prevent those educational gains from converting into representation.

The core argument

The United States is an outlier among wealthy democracies—not because American women lack education or ambition, but because specifically American institutional and cultural barriers prevent educational attainment from translating into political representation [5].

A timeline of education gains and the representation lag

The american paradox, and the potential paths forward

Our investigation reveals that this "American Paradox" is not a mystery of qualification, but a failure of infrastructure. While American women have successfully reversed the education gap, surpassing men in degrees and graduation rates, the political system has failed to capture this surplus of talent. Our comparison with peer nations like Canada and the Nordic countries confirms that the United States is an outlier—not by accident, but due to specific institutional and social dynamics that stifle representation.

The cost of this exclusion is high. As the data demonstrates, when women’s political power rises, economies innovate and grow. By allowing these barriers to persist, the US accepts not only a democratic deficit but a significant economic drawback. A Congress that does not reflect its educated majority cannot fully represent the American people.

The solution requires a dual approach: structural reform to our electoral pipelines and a cultural shift in how we value female leadership. As anthropologist Yolanda Moses argues, opportunity gaps are solved through policy, not just patience.

The Bottom Line

Women are not lacking in ambition or qualification; they are blocked by design. To close the gap, we must stop asking women to do more, and start building a system that lets them lead.

The education gap

Women now earn the majority of degrees

Since the 1980s, American women have achieved what economist Claudia Goldin calls a "reversal of the college attainment gap." Women are now more likely than men to earn bachelors and masters degrees. This represents a historic shift in educational access and achievement. Young women vote at rates 5.8 times higher than young men, showing strong civic engagement [12].

On the supply-side, the pool of educated, qualified candidates among the American women are not lacking. They have credentials, ambition, and voter engagement that far exceeds that of men. Yet this educational advantage has not translated into proportional political power. Universities and postsecondary institutions can help close this gap by institutionalizing internships, alumni-sponsorships, and campus fellowships that link graduates into political networks [31], and program evaluations indicate mentoring and targeted career supports improve recruitment into public-facing roles [31].

The question becomes: what happens on the demand-side? [5]

Beyond aggregate counts, recent literature shows where the pipeline frays. Longitudinal studies find many women exit high‑visibility STEM or corporate tracks into education and health sectors within a decade of graduation, reducing long-term access to political sponsorship networks [23]. Policy briefs and empirical syntheses argue that occupational segregation lowers public visibility and donor linkages for women, meaning supply-side credentials are necessary but not sufficient for nomination probability [31]. Complementary workplace-network research and self-efficacy experiments further clarify how network access and subjective confidence jointly shape who recruiters notice and whom parties consider for office [21].

Figure 1: Women's share of bachelors and masters degrees, 1993–2023. Women crossed the 50% threshold in the late 1980s and have remained the majority since [1].

Figure 1 shows a clear, sustained shift: women moved from parity to a durable majority of degree-holders by the late 1980s and have kept that advantage into the 2020s. This is not a short-lived blip but a structural change in who attains higher education in the United States [1].

That sustained majority matters for the "supply-side" argument: there are simply more highly educated women now than men. That enlarges the pool of potential candidates with credentials that, in many democracies, would be expected to convert into political representation [1].

But degrees take time to translate into political careers. Many legislators are mid-career or older: the pipeline from degree to elected office typically runs through professional, civic, and party networks that develop over decades. The time lag between rising educational attainment and visible political power helps explain part of the gap. However, it does not explain decades-long underrepresentation by itself. Empirical work finds that political ambition, sponsorship, and workplace networks, rather than qualifications alone, are able to drive candidacy and nomination outcomes [18].

Importantly, the curve in Figure 1 does not tell us where graduates work, which fields they enter, or how their careers interact with political recruitment. A majority of degrees does not automatically change who parties recruit, who donors back, or how voters perceive candidates [23].

Taken together, the time-series confirms a central project claim: supply-side strength is real and longstanding. The remaining question is why the demand-side, such as the institutions, party practices, and cultural attitudes, has not matched that supply.

Figure 2: Female share of enrollment vs. graduates: US vs. Canada comparison. Both countries show women enrolling at ~55%, but varying completion rates—Canada hovering near 60%, while the US climbs toward that threshold. This direct comparison is key: if Canada achieves 34% female MPs with similar educational demographics to the US (which has only 28%), education alone cannot explain the representation gap.

The US–Canada comparison isolates an important puzzle: similar enrollment and degree shares do not produce similar political outcomes. Canada outperforms the U.S. in female legislative representation even though both countries show comparable female shares among students and graduates [3].

This finding shifts attention from individual qualifications to the institutional choices that shape candidacy and election. If two countries start from similar educational inputs but diverge in representation, the difference is likely found in rules, party recruitment strategies, and public norms that either welcome or block women as viable candidates.

Another important nuance is completion versus enrollment. Enrollment is an early indicator; completion reflects persistence and supports a stable pool of qualified adults. The Canadian pattern, which is slightly higher completion, suggests that post-enrollment conditions (student supports, labor market attachments, family policies) can deepen the pipeline, which in turn interacts with political opportunity structures [11].

Policy differences, such as paid family leave, childcare, candidate quotas or centralized party recruiting, may help explain why similar educational inputs lead to different outputs. These are precisely the kinds of demand-side mechanisms that can be reformed to improve representation.

In short, Figure 2 strengthens the argument that education is necessary but not sufficient: institutional design and social supports determine whether education translates into seats.

Geographic variation, and education across the united states

Women represent the majority of undergraduate enrollees across nearly every US state, ranging from 49–69%. This geographic consistency shows that women's educational advantage is not concentrated in a few "liberal" states. It is actually nationwide.

State-level enrollment and completion patterns also interact with local labor markets and policy environments; longitudinal cohort work shows that regional labor demand and postsecondary supports affect long-run career trajectories for women, which in turn shape local pipelines into public life [23].

Figure 3: Women's share of undergraduate enrollment by US state, 2023–2024. Women represent 49–69% of undergraduate enrollees across all regions, demonstrating that female educational strength is a national phenomenon, not regional.

Figure 3 maps the impressive breadth of women's educational gains: nearly every state shows female majority enrollment, and the range (roughly 49–69%) demonstrates substantial state-level variation. This is not a pattern confined to coastal liberal enclaves. We can see that it is broadly distributed across regions.

State-level variation matters because political recruitment and opportunity structures are often organized at the state level. Party organizations, primary systems, and state legislatures set the first gatekeeping rules for aspiring candidates. High female enrollment in a state does not guarantee that state parties will recruit or support female contenders.

The map also points to strategic opportunities: states with higher female enrollment could be fertile ground for targeted recruitment, training programs, and candidate pipelines. Where enrollment and completion are high, short-term investments in recruitment or mentoring might yield visible gains in state legislatures and, later, federal delegations.

Conversely, states with lower female enrollment may need more upstream interventions such as college access, retention supports, and economic policies that keep educated women in public-facing careers. Addressing representation therefore requires a mix of statewide and national strategies tailored to local conditions.

Overall, the choropleth reinforces a core point: women's educational strength is nationwide, which makes the national representation shortfall even more striking and points policymakers toward state-focused levers as practical entry points for change.

Field of study, where women concentrate

Women's educational gains are not evenly distributed across all fields. The chart below shows women's share of enrollment in major fields of study. While women dominate health, education, and humanities fields, they remain significantly underrepresented in math, computer science, and engineering.

Field segregation is consequential: syntheses of labor-market evidence link occupational segregation to lower visibility and fewer donor or sponsor ties for women—patterns that reduce the likelihood of political recruitment from some fields [31].

Figure 4: Canada - Field of Study Women Share 2023. Shows the distribution of women across major fields in Canada, highlighting areas of concentration and underrepresentation.

Figure 5: United States - Field of Study Women Share 2024. Shows the distribution of women across major fields in the US, highlighting areas of concentration and underrepresentation.

Figures 4 and 5 expose a different dimension of the supply-side: field segregation. Women dominate health, education, and many social sciences, while they remain strongly underrepresented in engineering, computer science, and some finance tracks [31].

Field choices shape professional networks, earnings trajectories, and the types of public visibility that feed political careers. Women concentrated in caregiving and education fields may build different career-to-politics pathways than men concentrated in high-paying STEM or corporate leadership roles—paths that historically have provided easier access to donor networks and party elites. Longitudinal work on STEM persistence and occupational segregation shows women often transition out of STEM into education and health, reducing their pipeline access to high‑visibility networks [23].

These occupational shifts have direct implications for recruitment: registry-based research and employer-linked studies demonstrate that workplace ties to current officeholders and staff are powerful predictors of nomination success, which disadvantages those concentrated in lower-visibility sectors [21]. Where universities and regional colleges maintain alumni pipelines into public service, graduates see higher rates of political emergence—an institutional lever universities could strengthen to convert high female enrollment into candidacy pipelines [31].

This segregation therefore matters for representation: it helps explain why a numerical majority of graduates does not immediately translate into equal access to political pipelines that favor certain occupational backgrounds. Candidate recruitment often privileges those with prior public profiles, corporate ties, or fundraising experience—areas where men remain overrepresented.

Policy responses can target these structural effects: leadership training for women in underrepresented fields, incentives for parties to recruit across occupational backgrounds, and programs that increase the public visibility of women leaders from diverse fields can all help broaden the pool of viable candidates.

Ultimately, these figures show that improving representation requires attention not only to how many women graduate, but to where they graduate into—what careers, networks, and public roles those degrees enable.

The takeaway

American women are highly educated and academically prepared. They earn degrees at higher rates than men, excel across most academic fields, and show strong civic engagement. This is what political scientists call the supply-side of political participation—the existence of capable, willing candidates.

These supply-side facts are well documented in cohort and comparative studies of educational attainment and turnout [1]; [12], reinforcing that the core puzzle is not access to education but what happens after graduation.

On paper, the supply pipeline is strong. Yet supply alone does not produce representation. The problem is not women's education—the problem lies in what happens next. That is the demand-side: institutions, gatekeeping, and culture.

The representation gap: demand-side failure

The paradox: that simply because there is supply, seats are not automatically created

Despite having one of the highest rates of female educational attainment among wealthy democracies, the United States has only 28% female representation in Congress—worse than Canada, Germany, and the Nordic countries [5].

Political scientist Pamela Paxton argues that representation depends on two factors [5]:

  • Supply-side: Education, socioeconomic status, and women's desire for office — strong in the US
  • Demand-side: Institutional and cultural support for female politicians — weak in the US

[5]

Figure 6: The paradox: Female enrollment and graduates are nearly identical between the US and Canada—even slightly higher in the US—yet Canada has 34% female MPs versus the US's 28% in Congress.

The united states lags because of its institutional and cultural barriers

The demand-side problem is both institutional and cultural. Electoral systems, party recruitment, campaign finance rules, and prevailing attitudes all affect whether educated women become legislators [7].

New empirical work on party gatekeeping and nomination bias shows how institutional practices translate into unequal representation: parties sometimes reward candidates who match gendered expectations about family and career, and they may allocate women to less competitive seats, lowering aggregate female success independently of candidate quality [19]. Complementary workplace-network analyses and program evaluations further document how occupational ties and mentorship programs alter recruitment prospects, showing concrete entry points for policy intervention [21].

Case study: Italy, 1993. Electoral reform matters. Italy switched from a single-member district majoritarian electoral system to a mixed proportional representation system. Women's representation in parliament suddenly increased—not because women became more qualified, but because the rules changed. This research [2] shows that institutional design is crucial.

Figure 7: Mean percent women in parliament by electoral system, 2023. Countries using proportional representation (PR) average 27% female representation with wide variation; countries with other systems (including the US majoritarian model) average 25.7%. Error bars reveal: institutional rules matter. PR systems show both higher mean representation and more consistent outcomes across democracies.

This comparison directly supports the Profeta & Woodhouse (2022) [2] finding that proportional representation creates structural incentives for female candidacy and success that majoritarian systems lack. In PR systems, parties need to build broader coalitions and represent diverse constituencies, making gender diversity a strategic asset. In the US House and Senate, winner-take-all elections in single districts create gatekeeping dynamics where party leadership retains tight control over recruitment, and informal norms often favor male candidates [5]. Recent work on party gatekeeping and biased nominations explains how parties systematically disadvantage women—by rewarding candidates who fit traditional gendered family schemas or by nominating women in unwinnable seats [19].

However, in mature democracies like the United States, research suggests that cultural attitudes and norms are often the deeper barrier beyond institutional design [7]. Discrimination, stereotypes about female leaders, media representation, and informal party gatekeeping all limit women's path to office—even when formal rules are equal [5]. This explains why the US, despite having majoritarian systems like the UK (which also has 34% female MPs), still lags: American political culture has not embraced female leadership to the degree that it has elsewhere.

The research consensus: In new democracies, fix the electoral system [2]. In mature democracies like the US, cultural change is the priority [8].

College completion gaps: persistent female advantage

Even at the undergraduate level, women complete degrees at higher rates than men across most US states. In Nevada, Florida, and New York, women's 4-year completion advantages range from 4–9 percentage points. This is supply-side strength—yet it does not translate into political office.

Figure 8: College completion gender gap by state (2-year and 4-year cohorts). In states where women hold the highest completion advantage (Nevada: 7.3 pp, Florida: 5.8 pp), they still remain significantly underrepresented in statehouses and Congress. This underscores that completion rates do not predict representation.

The congressional pipeline has been stalled, and there has only been 35 years of glacial progress

Figure 9: Women's share of US Congress, 1990–2025 (filled area). Starting from 6.1% in 1990, women reached 28% by 2024—a gain of 22 percentage points over 35 years. At this rate, gender parity (50%) would not be achieved until roughly 2100, despite women being the majority of college graduates since the 1980s.

This visualization powerfully illustrates the core paradox of this project. Educational gains accelerated sharply in the 1980s–1990s (as shown in the Education Gap page), yet congressional representation remained nearly flat for a decade (1990–2000), crawled forward in the 2000s, and only recently accelerated post-2018. The mismatch between the trajectory of women's educational attainment (approaching 60% of graduates by 2020) and the rate of political representation gain (28% in 2024) is stark and undeniable. If education and qualification alone drove representation, women would hold approximately 50% of seats today—yet they hold barely more than half that share [6].

Intersectional and network-focused evidence helps explain this lag: registry and policy-brief research on occupational segregation and race indicates that women of color face compounded barriers that reduce nomination and election rates even further, implying that national aggregates hide distinct subgroup dynamics [21].

The stagnation of the 1990s and 2000s, despite the "Year of the Woman" spike in 1992[18], reveals what political scientists call demand-side failure. Individual female candidates became more qualified, more educated, and more present in state legislatures and local politics—the pipeline. Yet party recruitment, campaign finance dynamics, voter preferences, and media framing—the institutional and cultural forces on the demand-side— have not shifted fast enough to convert qualification into office-holding. Research by Profeta & Woodhouse [2] on electoral system design, and Welzel et al. [7] on cultural attitudes, suggests that both structural reforms and cultural shifts are necessary to accelerate representation gains.

The recent spike visible in 2018–2024 (from 19% to 28%) may signal an inflection point, but even this acceleration falls short of proportionality. Women entered Congress at record rates during this period, yet the share still represents only a slight majority of women's presence in higher education. This suggests that the barriers of gatekeeping, informal norms, and electoral incentives remain significant, even as public and party attitudes begin to shift [6].

The core finding

More education does not automatically create more seats. Women in the United States are not lacking qualifications—they are blocked by gatekeeping, norms, and systemic barriers. This is why the US remains an outlier despite its educated female population. The proof: Canada has similar female educational attainment to the US, yet achieves 34% female MPs versus the US's 28% in Congress.

Economy & growth

The issue is involved with economic growth

Research shows that female legislative representation is not just a fairness issue—it is an economic issue. When women hold political power, economies grow faster and innovation accelerates [10].

Economist Sirianne Dahlum studied 182 countries over 221 years and found a powerful correlation (R² = 0.95): when women have political power, per-capita GDP grows. Why? Because diverse leadership brings diverse ideas into the economy, leading to better policy and faster technological innovation [3].

More recent cross‑national panels and micro‑level studies unpack mechanisms: female policymakers tend to prioritize public education, family policies, and redistribution that raise human capital and labor force participation [9]. Policy‑oriented studies show that allowing campaign funds for childcare or providing candidate-support programs measurably increases female candidacy, which suggests administratively tractable demand‑side fixes with economic payoffs [31]. Comparative evidence from Canada and other countries shows that institutional design combined with active party recruitment yields both higher female representation and downstream economic benefits [3].

Program evaluations and case studies corroborate this: mentoring, placement, and candidate-support initiatives materially increase women's recruitment and short-term electoral success, indicating feasible policy levers with measurable downstream economic returns [31].

Figure 10: Women's representation in national parliaments vs. GDP per capita (n=228 countries, current US, log scale). Positive but non-deterministic correlation: wealthy democracies cluster high on both axes, while some lower-income countries achieve high female representation through quota systems or PR reforms. The USA is notably below the trend line for its income level, suggesting both economic underperformance relative to comparable peers and institutional constraints on female representation.

This global comparison is crucial. The scatter plot reveals that while wealth and female representation are positively associated [3]; [4], they are not perfectly correlated—suggesting that policy and institutional choice matter independently of economic development. Rwanda, for instance, achieved 61% female parliamentary representation through quota policies, while maintaining a much lower GDP per capita than the US. Conversely, the US sits notably below the trend line, indicating that for its income level, it underperforms on female representation. This positioning reveals a dual failure: the US economy has not maximized the innovation and policy benefits that higher female representation could bring [3], and American institutions have not yet adopted the policy tools (quotas, PR, campaign finance reform) that other democracies use to increase female candidacy and success.

Research by Jayasuriya & Burke[4] on 119 democracies (1970–2009) found that increasing women's representation in parliament directly leads to higher per-capita GDP growth. The mechanism operates through multiple channels: female legislators prioritize education and family support policies that raise human capital (Chen, 2021[9]); they bring new perspectives to economic policy that can reduce gender-based market inefficiencies; and they model leadership roles that encourage female labor force participation and entrepreneurship (Naveed et al., 2023[10]; Sirianne Dahlum et al., 2022[3]).

And scholars Park and Liang (2019) document a powerful feedback loop: female legislators invest in education and support for women, creating more educated female voters and future leaders. This cycles back to strengthen the supply-side even further. Chen (2021)[9] shows this concretely—the fraction of women in government correlates strongly to educational expenditures. Women in power invest in education, which boosts women's attainment, which creates more female leaders—a positive cycle that countries like Canada and Nordic nations have activated more fully than the US (Dahlum et al., 2022[3]).

For the United States specifically, the gap between current female representation (28%) and the global trend for a country of its income level (estimated ~35–40% based on [3]) suggests concrete economic opportunity costs: faster GDP growth, higher innovation rates, and more efficient policy design could result from closing the representation gap. This is not a speculative claim; it is a quantified pattern across democracies [4]; [3].

The US is Leaving Economic Growth on the Table

The United States is excluding a significant portion of its educated population from political power. This is not just a representation problem—it is an economic problem. By failing to use women's talents in governance, the US is forgoing measurable increases in innovation, policy quality, and GDP growth. If Canada, Germany, and Nordic countries have achieved higher female representation and enjoy economic benefits, the US has both a fairness obligation and an economic incentive to close the gap.

See cross‑national evidence linking women's political empowerment to later GDP growth [3] and comparative reports on Canada for institutional explanations [3].

What has to change?

Yolanda Moses, an anthropologist and education scholar, argues that "opportunity gaps can be fixed through policy changes" (Moses, 2012[11]). This applies to women in Congress too. Recommendations include:

  • Structural reforms: Campaign finance regulation, party quota systems, ranked-choice voting, or proportional representation—lessons from Italy's 1993 reform [2].
  • Cultural shifts: Mentorship programs for women, improved media representation of female leaders, and normalization of female ambition in politics [31].
  • Data-driven accountability: Track female recruitment, retention, and success rates in party pipelines; monitor quota enforcement and program impacts to avoid symbolic gains [2].

The bottom line: The problem is not that women lack education or ambition. The problem is that American systems and culture have not caught up to American women's capabilities. And that can change.

About, methods, & data critique

About: sources / processing / presentation

This section aligns our project to the three DH layers, citing Paxton, Kunovich, and Hughes as a guiding reading on how supply-side data and demand-side structures interact in gendered politics [5]. Each of the three layers—Sources, Processing, and Presentation—is discussed substantively below, including both technical details and the rationale for our decisions.

Sources

In terms of our sources, we focused on gathering peer-reviewed journals and previous scholarship that provided context for the existing factors and limitations contributing to the current gap between women's higher education and political representation, with an emphasis on an intersectional lens. We used these to examine the relationship between women's political participation and various socioeconomic and cultural conditions.

Our project consists of several datasets from the World Bank Gender Data Portal, the World Development Indicators database, as well as the V-Dem dataset [D1, D4, D5]. Processing

In processing our data, our primary focus was on examining how gender influences political representation within the United States. Consequently, we excluded data from other countries to maintain a more targeted scope. We used Python (pandas) for wrangling, recoding country/year panels, and splitting thematic CSVs; Tableau for aggregation and visual encodings; and structured timeline data for KnightLab. We excluded charts that failed labeling or scale clarity to keep rhetorical integrity.

The V-Dem dataset relies on evaluations from experts in the field of democracy and representation, and while this provides valuable insight, it also introduces potential bias [D5]. Expert opinions may differ significantly from the lived experiences of individuals within each country, potentially shaping interpretations in ways that overlook local nuances. The use of numerical data in the World Bank Gender Data Portal often hides the context as to why there is a lack of representation of women within government for each individual country.

Additionally, much of the available data begins after 1994, limiting historical context and potentially affecting our analysis, as societal understandings of gender continue to evolve. Furthermore, our dataset does not include representation from non-binary or gender-fluid individuals, omitting an important demographic from our analysis. To address these limitations, we integrated secondary sources that provide additional context and help ensure a more inclusive understanding of gender representation.

Understanding the gender data portal: scope & limitations

The Gender Data Portal collects gender-disaggregated data from international organizations (such as the IPU, ILO, UNESCO, WHO, and UNICEF) and national government agencies [D1, D2]. Key indicators include the proportion of female parliamentarians, women's literacy and education levels, labor force participation rates, the proportion of women in leadership positions, as well as access to health and technology. Data on political participation are based on the IPU's monthly rankings, which record the number of women in unicameral parliaments and lower houses. Data on education are combined from censuses, opinion surveys, and administrative records and processed according to UNESCO's methodology. The portal is updated quarterly, allowing for various analyses to be done upon the data.

This dataset identifies general patterns in gender inequality and enables comparative analysis across countries and time periods. It highlights regional differences (e.g., percentage of female parliamentarians: Americas 34.5% vs. Middle East and North Africa 16.7%) and tracks progress toward gender equality. Users can explore the relationship between women's education and their participation in the workforce, politics, and health. It also shows improving trends, with girls now outnumbering boys in secondary and tertiary education enrollment in many regions. This data is valuable for policymakers to justify equality efforts and for researchers analyzing macroeconomic and structural factors affecting women.

However, the most serious flaw is the gender classification system. Based on sex at birth, this system uses a binary male/female framework and ignores transgender people, non-binary women, and other gender-diverse individuals. Collecting data on these identities, especially in criminalized cases, is challenging, leading to significant underreporting.

Furthermore, this dataset lacks intersectionality. While it includes gender breakdowns, it fails to reflect the inequalities faced by racialized women, immigrants, and people with disabilities. This hinders analysis of the specific barriers they face and potentially perpetuates inequalities that favor privileged women.

This dataset does not include unpaid care work for children and families, which severely hinders women's economic and political participation. This work is not considered work in traditional statistics, GDP, or labor force participation rates. This portal cannot quantify the value of care work or show how it limits paid work and political activity. Research has shown that unpaid care work is a significant structural variable that limits women's ability to run for public office.

The dataset also fails to adequately reflect the informal economy, which is essential in low-income countries. Women's participation in the informal economy and domestic work is often underestimated, creating gaps in formal employment and distorting women's economic realities.

Furthermore, this portal lacks qualitative data on women's lived experiences and structural barriers. Numerical data on political participation fails to explain why women are underrepresented due to factors such as discrimination, violence, partisan bias, and cultural norms, limiting our understanding of how inequality persists.

Data generation, quality, & information gaps

Governments (population and household surveys) and international organizations use standardized tools to collect data. Data collection involves interviews and communication between national statistical offices, providing metadata to document sources and methods. However, data quality and availability vary widely, resulting in "data gaps," particularly in low- and middle-income countries. Many IDA-supported countries have weak civil registration and vital statistics systems, hindering understanding. When up-to-date data are unavailable, model estimates (projections) should be used. While standardization facilitates comparisons, it can overlook contextual aspects of inequality and regional variations in definitions (e.g., "employment").

Key data sources include national statistical offices, censuses, household surveys (e.g., DHS), administrative records (e.g., education, health, labor), and international organizations (political data) such as the Inter-Parliamentary Union (IPU). Major donors supporting gender data collection include Canada, Sweden, and the UK. The World Bank (Gender Group and DECDG) operates a portal and implements the Strengthening Gender Statistics Project (SGS Project) to support low-income countries. Globally, gender data is underfunded. Building a core system would cost approximately $500 million per year, nearly double current funding. Less than 0.5% of funding for COVID-19 response data is gender-related.

Several information types are systematically excluded. The dataset lacks data on violence against women (e.g., sexual harassment, intimate partner violence, and political violence), which poses a major obstacle. Social norms, cultural attitudes, and gender ideology are not directly measured (although external datasets such as the World Values Survey provide indirect indicators). The portal also lacks information on infrastructure that affects participation, such as access to childcare and public transport. Data on discrimination in hiring, promotion, and credit are also missing, as is information on women's political networks and mentoring. Furthermore, it does not reflect the quality of policies. Gender quotas may be recorded, but their effectiveness and implementation are not.

Focusing on quantifiable indicators like parliamentary representation and literacy rates prioritizes statistical analysis to the marginalization of other knowledge. This "tyranny of quantification" obscures qualitative aspects essential to understanding inequality, such as subjective discrimination, emotional labor, and internalized norms. If this were the only source of information, we might know the proportion of women in politics, but not the mechanisms behind it (party choice, voting bias, violence, caregiving barriers, etc.).

The dataset's focus on country-level aggregates also obscures intersectional inequalities. Treating "women" as a single category obscures how policies may benefit some women while disadvantaging women belonging to certain racial, ethnic, or religious groups. Research has shown that gender quotas can even reduce, rather than increase, the representation of minority women, and the World Bank's standard binary analysis fails to capture these dynamics. This effectively forces gender-based analysis over intersectional analysis, legitimizing this approach through its status as an authoritative database.

Methodology & visualization selection

We selected visualizations based on clarity and accuracy. Some charts in our analysis were excluded because they lacked clear labeling, contained empty data, or mixed incomparable scales. Our principle: only use graphics that directly support the argument and are interpretable by readers. To understand why the US lags in representation despite comparable female educational attainment, we examined the direct comparison of enrollment and graduation trends between the United States and Canada.

Reflecting on data as argument

Data is not neutral. How we define "education," "representation," and "success" reflects values and choices. By being transparent about our data, limitations, and exclusions, we aim to show that this is an argument constructed from evidence—not an objective truth discovered in numbers. Our argument is that the United States is an outlier and that change is possible. The data supports this. But the data also has gaps, and our humanistic reading of these gaps is part of the project's contribution.
