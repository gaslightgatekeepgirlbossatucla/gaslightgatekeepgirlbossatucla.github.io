Educated, but Unrepresented

American women now earn more bachelors and masters degrees than men. Yet, they hold only 28% of congressional seats. Consider this paradox to reveal a hidden truth: that education alone does not guarantee political power in our country.

60%

Currently, 60% of women in the United States have degrees. This increases the risk of a lack of representation. We need to do more and create a system that allows women to lead.

28%

Currently, women make up 28% of the total number of legislators, indicating that education professionals are still lagging behind in reaching legislative positions.

Our central questions

  • Why does the United States lag behind other wealthy democracies in terms of female political representation, in spite of having one of the highest female educational attainment rates and shares among developed nations?
  • Does women's power contribute to economic growth in America, as it does in other countries?
  • What institutional factors and cultural factors could explain the gap that has been seen in the United States?

Many scholars have studied how supply (highly educated women) changes to demand (becoming politicians). However, research shows that academic papers alone are not enough. The ability of people with educational backgrounds to run for political office in a country varies depending on psychological, peer, and governance factors. Questionnaire surveys have found significant differences between men and women who said they wanted to become politicians, even among those with the same intelligence level and educational background. [13]

Consider this: The way political parties select candidates suggests that who you know and who you help (not just money) are the reasons for choosing you. [16] Furthermore, qualitative and quantitative research on party gatekeeping reveals that selection and nomination are always based on outdated methods, making it difficult for female candidates. This is a demand-side obstacle. Even if many women have excellent educational backgrounds, obstacles remain. [14] There is also complementary research on workplace network studies and program evaluations, showing them to be game-changers and important levers for better recruitment outcomes and positive change. [15]

Why higher education does not guarantee political power

Since the 1980s, American women have achieved what economist Claudia Goldin calls the "college-going backslide." Women are now more likely than men to earn bachelor's and master's degrees. This represents a historic shift in educational access and election success. Young women vote at a rate 5.8 times higher than young men, signaling strong urban engagement. [12]

On the supply side, the pool of American women candidates is not lacking. They far outpace men in paperwork, aspirations, and voting engagement. However, this educational advantage has not translated into proportional political power. Universities and after-school education institutions can close this gap by connecting alumni to political networks through institutionalized internships, alumni sponsorships, and campus fellowships. [18] The value of these programs demonstrates that mentoring and targeted career support facilitate recruitment into public life. [18]

The question is, what's happening on the demand side? [7]

Beyond the total numbers, recent papers show where the pipes break. Longstanding research has found that many women leave prestigious science or business fields for education and healthcare within 10 years of graduation, reducing their long-term access to political sponsorship networks. [17] A mix of policy briefs and theory suggests that job turnover reduces women's social interests and donor connections, and that supply-side documentation is necessary but not sufficient for nomination opportunities. [18] Complementing workplace online surveys with experiments on self-power will further shed light on how internet access and participants' self-confidence combine to determine who recruiters focus on and who political parties select for office. [15]

In processing the data, we focused primarily on observing the influence of gender on the political makeup of the United States. Therefore, to expand our scope, we excluded other countries from the data. Python (Pandas) was used to organize country/year panels, recode, and extract thematic CSV files. Tableau data collection was used for visual coding, and KnightLab was used for the data's timeline. To maintain the integrity of the rhetorical approach, we excluded graphs with unclear naming or scale [D5].

The V-dem dataset relies on expert assessments in the fields of democracy and representation, which provide valuable insights but also introduce potential bias [D5]. Expert opinions vary significantly from the lived experiences of individuals in each country, with potential formulations being interpreted in ways that overlook local nuances. The World Bank's Gender Data Portal's use of numerical data often obscures context about why women's representation is low in government institutions across countries.

Furthermore, the available data begins after 1994, limiting historical context and potentially impacting our understanding of gender continuing to evolve in society. Furthermore, our dataset does not include the representation of non-binary or gender-fluid people, excluding an important group from our analysis. We address these limitations by incorporating a second source that provides additional context and helps ensure a more comprehensive understanding of gender representation.

This data analysis combines quantitative and humanistic interpretation. We approach data on political and educational indicators neutrally, not as evidence, but as structures, social structures, access, power, and so on. The goal of our understanding is to understand that systems whose outcomes differ simply produce what they do differently.

Our project consists of several datasets presented in the World Bank Gender Data Portal, the World Development Indicators database, and the V-Dem dataset. Specifically, we obtained data from indicators such as the percentage of women in parliament and GDP growth rates from the World Bank platform, and indicators of the level of democracy in a country, analyzing the impact of institutions, political participation, women's political role, and the V-Dem dataset on leadership. We also curated the bibliography and annotated the datasets to further understand the implications. We explored our referenced journals, numerous peer-reviewed and scholarly studies, previous context, and the assumption that existing factors and limitations of the current gap contributed to women's higher education levels between and within the political sphere, through an intersectional lens, with emphasis on equality, gender, and reviews.

The core argument

America is an outsider among wealthy democracies not because American women are uneducated or unmotivated, but because certain American institutions and cultural barriers prevent them from translating education into political stances. [5]

A timeline of education gains and the representation lag

The American Paradox, and the potential paths forward

Research shows that this "American paradox" is not a question of ability. Consider a structural analysis. While both women and men outperform men in degree attainment and completion rates, the education gap reduces success, and the political system cannot cope with this opportunity for additional gains. The United States' outlier performance can be compared to American-friendly countries such as Canada and Scandinavia. This is not a coincidence; underrepresentation is driven by specific institutions and social movements.[5]

The cost of this high level is limited. Data show that women's political influence increases as the economy develops and grows. This obstacle ignores this democratic deficit. Not only is the United States failing to secure a place, but the economy will also suffer serious setbacks. A congressional majority which ultimately does not reflect the will of theAmerican people cannot fully represent the American people.[3]

A two-part solution requires participation, addressing the injustices in elections, and reforming how women's leadership is valued. Anthropologist Yolanda Moses argues that the opportunity gap cannot be simply solved through policy.[11]

The bottom line

There's a gap. Women want skills, but they're not getting enough. They're lagging behind, holding back, but they simply exist. The gap is so close that we need to demand an end to women's participation and start creating systems that empower them, empower their initiatives, and embrace them.

The education gap

Women now earn the majority of degrees

Since the 1980s, American women have achieved what economist Claudia Goldin calls a "reversal of the college attainment gap." Women are currently earning more bachelor's and master's degrees in comparison to men. This represents a historic shift in educational access and achievement where women are leading. It is also evident that young women's voting rates are 5.8 times higher than young men's, which displays strong civic engagement [12].

On the supply side, the pool of educated, qualified candidates among American women is not lacking. These women have credentials, voter engagement, and above all ambition that far exceeds that of men. Yet this educational advantage has not been reflected proportionally within political power across Congressional seats. However, universities and postsecondary institutions can help mitigate this political gap by institutionalizing internships, alumni-sponsorships, and campus fellowships that link graduates into political networks [18], and program evaluations—especially with a prioritization on mentorship and personalized career support—improve recruitment for public-facing roles [18].

Now the question becomes: what happens on the demand-side? [7]

Beyond aggregate counts, recent scholarship shows where the pipeline frays. Longitudinal studies find many women exit high‑visibility STEM or corporate tracks into education and health sectors within a decade of graduation, reducing long-term access to political sponsorship networks [17]. Policy briefs and empirical syntheses argue that occupational segregation lowers public visibility and donor linkages for women, meaning supply-side credentials are necessary but not sufficient for nomination probability [18]. Complementary workplace-network research and self-efficacy experiments further clarify how network access and subjective confidence jointly shape who recruiters notice and whom parties consider for office [15].

To explore why academic achievement is not currently reflected within political power, this project leverages World Bank gender data to compare 170 countries from 1970 to the present [D1, D3, D4]. Through our analysis of this data we find that the U.S. holds a 28% legislative representation in comparison to other wealthy democracies who perform better such as: Canada ~34%, Germany ~35%, and the Nordic countries ~40–47% (World Bank). As such, we frame three questions central to the project: why the US is currently lacking in female representation, whether women's power contributes to economic growth in the US as seen in other democratic countries, and what institutional or cultural factors best explain the gap.

First, researchers such as Claudia Goldin and Pamela Paxton, Sheri Kunovich, and Melanie Hughes have shown that women now achieve greater educational attainment in wealthy countries [1]. However, higher education does not necessarily equal entry into political power: Paxton emphasizes a dual framework of the supply-side: education, socioeconomic status, and willingness to run; and the demand-side: institutional and cultural support drivers.

Secondly, scholars debate whether cultural attitudes or institutional rules matter most. Profeta & Woodhouse's comparative work uses Italy's 1993 electoral reform as evidence that electoral rules can sharply change representation even when candidate qualifications are unchanged [2]. Longitudinal work by Sirianne Dahlum and colleagues shows a positive association between women's political empowerment and later GDP per‑capita growth [3], while cross‑national panels such as Jayasuriya & Burke report similar links between female legislative share and later economic performance [4]. Park & Liang document the feedback loop: female legislators improve education and social outcomes that cultivate future female leaders [6].

Finally, research on democratic maturity argues that in less‑developed countries, institutional fixes like quota systems or proportional representation can produce rapid gains; in mature democracies cultural attitudes often matter more. See Tremblay [8] for a comparative perspective. In short, the U.S. appears to be an outlier, not because women lack the education or ambition needed, but because specific institutional and cultural barriers prevent these women from entering and gaining political power.

The plotted series underscores two critical facts: (1) Canada achieved the highest female graduate shares, reaching roughly 60% in the early 2000s and settling in the mid-50s thereafter. (2) On the other hand, the U.S. series (observed from approximately 2010 onward) rose from about 50% to near 54% by 2023. These changes represent sustained, not fleeting, shifts in graduates' gender composition [1].

This long-term shift strengthens the supply-side argument: there are significantly more highly educated women than men, and this advantage has persisted for decades. It expands the potential political candidate pool with the educational qualifications that support electoral viability in many democracies [1].

It is important to note that it takes time for degrees to become political power. Many legislators are mid-career or older, and the pathway from degree to elected office typically passes through professional, civic, and party networks built over years. This delay between rising education and visible political power accounts for a portion of the gap, but cannot alone explain the prolonged underrepresentation. Research demonstrates that political ambition, sponsorship, and career networks, in addition to credentials, drive candidacy and nomination success [13].

Importantly, the curves do not reveal where graduates work, their career fields, or how professional trajectories shape political recruitment opportunities. Educational majority status does not automatically determine party recruitment decisions, donor support, or voter perception of candidates [17].

Taken together, the figure confirms a core project assertion: supply-side strength is real and enduring. The central remaining question is why the demand-side like institutions, party practices, and cultural norms, have yet to align with this educational supply.

Figure 2: Enrollment rates (dotted) and completion rates (solid) for women in the United States and Canada. Enrollment rates in both countries hover around 55–56% for most years. Canada has a higher completion rate (around 57–60% in the 2000s), while the U.S. completion rate increased from around 50% (around 2010) to around 54% by 2023. This comparison highlights the point that identical enrollment rates do not guarantee identical political outcomes [3].

With a close look at both Canada and the US, our findings concluded that while the proportion of women enrolled in both countries is roughly similar (approximately 55–56%), their completion rates and political outcomes differ. Canada's completion rates tend to be higher (mid- to high-50s), while the United States' completion rates have remained in the mid-50s since 2010. Despite this slight difference in educational levels, Canada has significantly higher legislative representation, suggesting once again that educational attainment levels alone is not the only explanatory factor [3].

This finding shifts the focus from individual efforts to institutional choice and social support, which shape candidacy and election outcomes. If two countries start with similar educational levels but exhibit differences in representation, the explanation is likely to be found in electoral rules, party recruitment strategies, candidate support (childcare, parental leave), and public opinion, which affect who runs and who is selected as a candidate.

Another important point is the difference between enrollment and completion. Enrollment is an early indicator; completion reflects persistence and supports a stable pool of capable adults. Canada's pattern, with slightly higher completion rates, suggests post-enrollment conditions (student support, labor market attachment, and family policies) deepen the pipeline, which may interact with the political opportunity structure [11].

Geographic variation, and education across the united states

In nearly every US state, women account for roughly 49–69% of undergraduate enrollments each year. "Liberal" states are not the sole driver of women's educational strength; the pattern is national.

State-level enrollment and completion patterns link to local job markets and policy environments. Longitudinal research shows that employment demands and postsecondary support shape women's long-term career paths and their transitions from education into work and civic life [17].

Figure 3: Percentage of female undergraduate enrollment by US state, 2023–2024. Across all regions, females account for 49–69% of undergraduate enrollment, indicating that the strength of female education is national, not regional.

Figure 3 illustrates the scope of female educational gains. Nearly every state sees female majority enrollment, ranging from roughly 49% to 69%, with most states in the mid-50s. This is not a liberal enclave pattern, but a widespread national phenomenon.

State-level variation matters because political recruitment and opportunity structures are often organized at the state level. Party organizations, primary systems, and state legislatures set the first gatekeeping rules for aspiring candidates. High female enrollment in a state does not guarantee that state parties will recruit or support female candidates [17].

The map also points to strategic opportunities: states with higher female enrollment could be fertile ground for targeted recruitment, training programs, and candidate pipelines. Where enrollment and completion are high, short-term investments in recruitment and mentoring can yield tangible results in state legislatures and, later, in Congress [15].

Conversely, states with lower female enrollment may need upstream interventions to keep educated women in public-facing careers, such as college access, family support, and economic policies. Addressing representation therefore requires a mix of statewide and national strategies tailored to local conditions.

Overall, the map reinforces a core point: the strength of female education is national, which makes the national representation shortfall even more striking and points policymakers toward state-focused levers as practical entry points for change.

Field of study, where women concentrate

Women's academic achievement is uneven across fields. The charts below show the representation of women in major fields of study. While women dominate in health, education, and social sciences, they remain significantly underrepresented in mathematics, computer science, and engineering.

Field segregation has significant consequences. Occupational segregation and labor market barriers combine to make women less visible and less accessible to funders and sponsors, a pattern that reduces the likelihood of recruitment from certain fields [18].

Figure 4: Canada - Field of Study Women Share 2023. Women's share varies widely by field—roughly ~22% in engineering and some computer/technical fields, up to ~75–80% in education and health-related fields—highlighting clear areas of concentration and underrepresentation.

Figure 5: United States - Field of Study Women Share 2024. Women's shares span a wide range—about ~22–30% in computer, engineering, and some finance tracks, and very high shares (~75–90%) in nursing, health, and certain social science fields—underscoring how field segregation shapes occupational pipelines.

Figures 4 and 5 illustrate a clear dimension of women's advancement: field segregation. Women hold high shares in health, education, and many social science fields (often exceeding 70–80% in certain subfields), while remaining underrepresented in engineering, computer science, and some finance fields (often around 20–30%) [18].

These fields shape occupational pathways and public visibility that affect political careers. Women concentrated in caregiving and education often pursue career-to-politics pathways that differ from those of men concentrated in high-paid STEM or corporate leadership roles. These paths have historically provided easier access to donor networks and party elites. Longstanding research on STEM retention and occupational segregation shows that women transition from STEM to education and health sectors, narrowing their pipeline access to high-visibility networks [17].

These career transitions have direct implications for recruitment. Registry-based research and employer-linked studies demonstrate that workplace ties to current officeholders and staff are powerful predictors of nomination success, which disadvantages those concentrated in lower-visibility sectors [15]. Where universities and regional colleges maintain alumni pipelines into public service, graduates see higher rates of political emergence—an institutional lever universities could strengthen to convert high female enrollment into candidacy pipelines [18].

This segregation also matters for representation: it helps explain why a numerical majority of graduates does not immediately translate into equal access to political pipelines. Candidate recruitment often privileges those with corporate backgrounds, prior political experience, or fundraising networks—areas where men remain overrepresented.

Policy responses can target these structural effects: leadership training for women in underrepresented fields, candidate selection systems that value broader occupational backgrounds, and programs that increase the public visibility of women leaders from diverse fields can all help broaden the pool of viable candidates.

Ultimately, these figures show that improving representation requires attention not only to how many women graduate, but to where they graduate into—what careers, networks, and public roles those degrees enable.

The takeaway

American women are highly educated and well-prepared. They attain degrees at higher rates than men, excel in most academic fields, and actively participate in politics. This is what political scientists call the supply-side of political participation—the existence of qualified and motivated candidates.

These supply-side facts provide excellent documentation for comparative cohort studies of educational attainment and voter turnout [1][12], reinforcing that the core puzzle is not lack of access to education but rather what happens after graduation.

On paper, the supply pipeline is powerful. Yet supply alone does not produce representation. The problem is not women's lack of education—the problem lies in what happens next. That is the demand-side: institutions, gatekeeping, and culture.

Two major demand-side factors highlighted in the figures are occupational segregation (women are overrepresented in healthcare, education, and social services, and underrepresented in engineering, computer science, and finance) and the structure of party recruitment and campaign finance that privilege certain occupational networks—both factors that dampen the translation of educational gains into political candidacies [18].

The representation gap: demand-side failure

The paradox: that simply because there is supply, seats are not automatically created

Although the United States has one of the highest rates of women being elected to the political sphere based on their educational attainment among developed democracies, only 28% of its members in Congress are women, lower than those in Canada, Germany, and the Nordic countries.[7]

Political scientist Pamela Paxton argues that representation in Congress depends on two factors:[7]

  • The supply side: education, wealth, social status, and women's desire to run for office. These are strong in the United States.
  • The demand side: institutions and culture support women politicians. These are weak in the United States.[7]

Figure 6: Women (top row) and women (bottom row) in Canada and the United States. The representation of women has remained roughly the same (approximately 55-56%). While the representation of women is roughly the same, Canada has experienced a decline in its representation of women throughout the 2000s. These indicators highlight a large gap that cannot be explained by Canada alone, with women representing approximately 34% of legislators in Canada compared to approximately 28% in the U.S. Congress.

The united states lags because of its institutional and cultural barriers

Issues on the demand side stem from both institutions and culture. Voting systems, party recruitment, campaign finance, and historical attitudes all affect the election of educated women to parliament.[5]

New research on party gatekeeping and nomination bias reveals that institutional practices drive unequal representation in elections. Parties may prioritize family and careers and select gender-matched candidates. This can lead to women filling less competitive seats and lowering their electoral participation rates, regardless of the quality of the candidates.[14] Partners' workplace network analysis and program value further demonstrate how employment relations and mentoring programs can change future recruitment, providing a clear entry point for policy modification.[15]

Case Study: Italy in 1993. Rethinking voting systems is crucial. Italy moved from a single-member district system with majority rule to a mixed proportional representation system. While the number of women elected to parliament increased significantly, this was due to institutional changes, not to improved women's qualifications. This study demonstrates the critical importance of institutional design.[2]

Figure 7: Average proportion of women on voting machine courts in 2023. Countries with PR systems have a significantly different representation of women, averaging 27%. Countries with other systems (including the US Big Win system) have an average of 25.7%. The error shows that the rules of the system matter. PR systems show a higher median and more uniform results across democracies.

The United States excludes many academics from politics. This is not just a matter of appearances, but of money. By not utilizing female talent in politics, the United States misses out on tangible results in innovation, policy efficiency, and GDP growth. While Canada, Germany, and the Nordic countries have women in higher positions and enjoy financial benefits, the United States must fill the gap with both fairness and financial benefits.

Research shows that policy changes can close opportunity gaps.[11] This also applies to women in politics. Recommendations include:

Structural reform: funding rules, party allocation systems, ranked-choice voting, or percentage allocation, which are lessons from the 1993 Italian reform.[2]

Cultural reform: teacher training programs for women, increased media exposure for female leaders, and realizing women's aspirations in politics.[18]

Data-driven accountability: Tracking the recruitment, retention, and success of women in political parties. Monitor the implementation of quotas and programs to avoid false benefits.[2]

Conclusion: The problem isn't that women are uneducated or lack hope. The problem is that the American system and culture haven't kept pace with the power of American women. And that can be changed.

College completion gaps: persistent female advantage

In most US states, women finish their dissertations faster than men, even in their early years. In Nevada, Florida and New York, the four-year completion bonus for women ranges from 4 to 9 percent. This is supply-side power, but it has no political space.

Even in states with the highest female graduation rates (Nevada: 7.3%; Florida: 5.8%), women are still significantly underrepresented in state legislatures and representative offices, confirming the lack of change in graduation rates.

The congressional pipeline has been stalled, and there has only been 35 years of glacial progress

Figure 9: Percentage of women in the U.S. legal profession from 1990 to 2025. The percentage of women in the legal profession, which was approximately 6% in 1990, is projected to reach approximately 28% by 2024, representing an increase of approximately 22% over 35 years. A simple extrapolation of recent trends predicts that the percentage of women in the legal profession will remain roughly flat until around 2100, highlighting the gradual change that continues without any institutional reform to achieve this goal.

This figure elucidates a central mystery: While the number of women in education surged from the 1980s to the 1990s, the percentage of women in Congress remained roughly flat throughout the 1990s, increased slowly in the 2000s, and has shown a more pronounced increase since 2018. The imbalance, with some legislators approaching 60% women by 2020 (and some legislators remaining at approximately 28%), is alarming. If education alone dominated the seats, we would expect it to be roughly flat today, but its appearance remains well below its lowest point.[6]

Cross-sectional and net-focus evidence helps explain this lag: List and policy brief studies of occupational barriers and race show that women of color face layered barriers that further reduce nomination and voter turnout, suggesting that the nation as a whole is masking a distinct subgroup movement.[15]

Despite a surge in the "Year of the Woman" in 1992,[13] the stagnation of the 1990s and 2000s reveals what political scientists call the "collapse of the hope side." Women candidates became more qualified, better educated, and more visible in state and local politics (the so-called "tube"). However, party hiring, campaign finance, voter preferences, and news framing (i.e., the organizational and cultural power of the hope side) did not change quickly enough to translate qualifications into office acceptance. Research by Profeta and Woodhouse on voting system design[2] and research on cultural attitudes by Welzel et al.[5] suggest that both structural improvements and cultural changes are necessary to accelerate progress.

The recent surge—an expected increase in women's representation from 19% to 28% between 2018 and 2024—might mark a turning point, but even this rate of increase falls short of the target. During this period, women will advance into parliament at record rates, yet women remain only a majority in higher education. This suggests that even as public and party attitudes begin to change, barriers of door guards, casual norms, and preferential voting practices remain significant.[6]

The core finding

More schools don't automatically mean more chairs. American women don't lose their papers. They're held back by gatekeepers, rules, and systems. That's why we remain outsiders, even though we have women in our schools. Proof of this is that Canada, despite having more girls' schools than the US, has 34% women in its legislature, compared to 28% in the US.

Economy & growth

The issue is involved with economic growth

Research shows that advancing women into the legal profession isn't just a matter of equity; it's also a financial one. When women have political power, economic growth accelerates and innovation accelerates.[10]

Money scientist Cillian Durham studied 182 countries over a 221-year period and found a strong correlation (r² = 0.95): When women have political power, GDP per capita increases. Why? Because gender equality leads to gender equality in the economy, which leads to better policies and faster technological innovation.[3]

A new international panel and small-scale study show that female policymakers prioritize public schools, family policies, and financial transfers that promote human values and workforce participation.[9] Policy research suggests that providing financial assistance for childcare and candidate support programs measurably increases female candidate aspirations and delivers financial results.[18] Comparisons between Canada and other countries show that by partnering with active political party recruiting organizations, promoting gender equality can achieve both: making women appear taller and downstream financial benefits.[3]

The value of the program and supporting case studies: education, placement, and candidate support materials suggest actionable policy responses that increase women's employment and short-term voter turnout, resulting in measurable downstream financial benefits.[18]

The US population, being highly educated, eliminates the influence of political power. The problem of representation, as such, is not an economic issue. Managing the talents of women, harnessing their power to achieve success in the US, is a significant innovation in increasing measurable gains in policy quality and GDP growth [3][4].

Canada, Germany, and the Nordic countries, while having higher female representation, also have economic advantages, benefiting from a commitment to fairness while the US commits to creating economic disparities that feel unfair.

Burke and Jayasuriya's study of democracies (1970-2009) [4] shows that women's representation in parliament increases, and per capita income increases, which directly leads to this. Various channels of communication through the governance mechanism contribute to this. Family and school support policies prioritize legislators and promote women's values.[9] Market differences, gender differences, and narrow financial prospects can bring them new ones. Women promote this leadership model through entrepreneurship, participation, and labor.[10][3]

The feedback loop is powerful, documented by researchers: future leaders and voters—women with higher education—support legislators by supporting and spending on education.[6] Supply-side dynamism stimulates a further cycle.[9] Education spending significantly contributes to women's role. Education is invested in, and women are influential. When women's status improves, this cycle is positive, and female leaders, whose numbers increase, in turn contribute to this cycle. The Nordic countries and Canada, like countries in the United States, are more resilient.[3]

Only the United States (28%), women's representation, the current gap between the status of the economic country, and the global perspective (based on 35-40% estimates) [3] economic losses are tangible for potential people, suggests higher GDP growth to lead to reduced inequality, lower unemployment, and more effective policymaking. This is not a fanciful assertion, but that this is true, societies are democratic according to the observed model.[4][3]

The US is Leaving Economic Growth on the Table

The United States is excluding a significant portion of its educated population from political power. This is not just a representation problem—it is an economic problem. By failing to use women's talents in governance, the US is forgoing measurable increases in innovation, policy quality, and GDP growth. If Canada, Germany, and Nordic countries have achieved higher female representation and enjoy economic benefits, the US has both a fairness obligation and an economic incentive to close the gap.

See cross‑national evidence linking women's political empowerment to later GDP growth [3] and comparative reports on Canada for institutional explanations [3].

What has to change?

Research shows that opportunity gaps can be fixed through policy changes[11]. This applies to women in Congress. Recommendations include:

  • Structural reforms: Campaign finance regulation, party quota systems, ranked-choice voting, or proportional representation—lessons from Italy's 1993 reform [2].
  • Cultural shifts: Mentorship programs for women, improved media representation of female leaders, and normalization of female ambition in politics [18].
  • Data-driven accountability: Track female recruitment, retention, and success rates in party pipelines; monitor quota enforcement and program impacts to avoid symbolic gains [2].

The bottom line: The problem is not that women lack education or ambition. The problem is that American systems and culture have not caught up to American women's capabilities. And that can change.

About, methods, & data critique

About: sources / processing / presentation

In this section, we cite Paxton, Kunovich, and Hughes's paper as a guiding reference on the integration of supply-side data and demand-side construction in gender politics.[7] We organize this project into three layers. Below, we discuss each of these three layers—sources, preparation, and presentation—in detail, including technical details and the rationale for our decisions.

Sources

With regard to sources, we focus on collecting peer-reviewed literature and previous studies that provide context for the existing factors and limitations contributing to the current gap between high school girls and political activism, with a particular emphasis on an intersectional lens. Using these, we explored the relationship between girls' political participation and various economic, social, and cultural conditions.

This project is comprised of multiple datasets from the World Bank Gender Data Portal, the World Development Indicators Base, and the V-DEM dataset [D1]. Specifically, we obtain data from these datasets on the proportion of women in the legal profession, GDP growth rates on the World Bank platform, and country-level democracy. We extract indicators from the V-DEM dataset to analyze the impact of political institutions on women's political participation and leadership roles.

Additionally, we curated a note-biblio to gain a deeper understanding of the primary dataset. This study used numerous papers and reports on gender identity, political participation, the relationship between women's representation in the legal profession and economic growth, academic success, and cultural factors that influence women's participation in society in various countries. These materials range from research papers, surveys, and reviews related to the Gender Identity Project. This study covers the period from 1990 to the present, which provides the most comprehensive and comparable data.

Processing

In processing the data, we primarily focused on observing how gender influences political appearance in the United States. Therefore, to broaden our scope, we excluded data from other countries. We used Python (Pandas) to organize country/year panels, recode, and extract thematic CSVs. Tableau was used for data collection and visual coding, and KnightLab was used to structure the timeline data. To maintain rhetorical integrity, we excluded graphs with unclear naming or scale.

The v-dem dataset relies on expert assessments in the fields of democracy and appearance, which provides valuable insights but also introduces potential bias [D5]. Expert opinions can differ significantly from the lived experiences of individuals in each country, leading to potential formulations being interpreted in ways that overlook local nuances. The World Bank's Gender Data Portal's use of numerical data often obscures context about why women's appearances are low in government institutions across countries.

Additionally, much of the available data begins after 1994, limiting historical context and potentially impacting our analysis as society's understanding of gender continues to evolve. Furthermore, our data set does not include the appearance of non-binary or gender-fluid people, excluding an important group from our analysis. To address these limitations, we incorporate a second source that provides additional context and helps ensure a more comprehensive understanding of gender appearance.

Presentation

For our project, "Educated, but Unrepresented," we decided to publish an interactive web-based story on GitHub Pages. We used a lightweight, custom single-page application structure and vanilla JavaScript. Instead of reloading the browser every time you navigate between pages, we use DOM manipulation to instantly switch active content sections (Home, Education, Representation, etc.). This creates a very responsive, app-like user experience.

We used Python extensively for data processing and visualization. Our toolkit included pandas for data manipulation, grouping, merging, and transformation; numpy for numerical operations; matplotlib and seaborn for creating static visualizations (line plots, bar charts, histograms); geopandas with shapely for geospatial data handling and choropleth map generation; and requests and urllib for fetching data from web APIs. We also leveraged scipy, pypeds, and custom ETL scripts to extract and transform educational attainment data from IPEDS, World Bank, and Census APIs. We then exported our visualizations as static images and embedded them within the site. We also used Tableau Public extensively to create polished interactive graphs, dashboards, and maps (including state choropleths, field-of-study comparisons, and cross-national analyses), which we embedded as iframes for full interactivity and exploration. We included an interactive timeline (Timeline JS - Knight Lab) to illustrate the time lag between improved education and political representation.

We used a color palette and high-contrast text to convey a serious tone. We used Playfair Display (a serif font) for headlines to create an editorial, journalistic feel, and Inter (a sans-serif font) for body copy to ensure readability. We also took inspiration from Glassmorphism, giving the navigation a modern frosted glass effect that is easy to see and doesn't block content.

We also created a custom JavaScript algorithm that auto-detects superscripts in citations (e.g., [1]) and links directly to the bibliography entry, highlighting sources for users. The site uses CSS Grid and Flexbox in combination with media queries to ensure our data stories are easy to read on any device, from mobile to large desktop monitors. This layer operationalizes the demand-side framing from Paxton et al. [7] by pairing quantitative evidence with explanatory text.

Project approach & data sources

This project combines quantitative data analysis with humanistic interpretation. We treat educational and political data not as neutral numbers, but as evidence of social structures, access, and power. Our goal is to understand why systems produce different outcomes, not just that they do.

Our project consists of several datasets from the World Bank Gender Data Portal, the World Development Indicators database, as well as the V-Dem dataset [D1, D4, D5]. Specifically, we obtained data from these sources on indicators such as percentage of women in parliament and GDP growth rates on the World Bank platform, and country-level democracy indicators to analyze the impact of political institutions on women's role in political participation and leadership from the V-Dem dataset. Additionally, we curated an annotated bibliography to understand our primary datasets further. Our study used numerous peer-reviewed journals and previous scholarship that provided context for the existing factors and limitations that contributed to the current gap between women in higher education and the political sphere, with an emphasis through an intersectional lens. These materials ranged from research, surveys, and reviews of gender equality projects.

Understanding the gender data portal: scope & limitations

The Gender Data Portal collects gender-disaggregated data from United Nations (IPU, ILO, UNESCO, WHO, UNICEF, etc.) and national government agencies [D1]. Key indicators include the percentage of women in the legal profession, women's literacy and educational attainment, labor force participation rates, the percentage of women in managerial positions, and entry into the health and technology sectors. Data on political participation is based on the IPU's monthly rankings, which show the number of women in the Senate and House of Representatives. Data on education is compiled from headcounts, opinion polls, and administrative records, following the UNESCO methodology. The Portal is updated four times a year, allowing for a variety of analyses based on the data.

This data panel shows overall trends in gender equality and enables comparative analysis across countries and time periods. It examines progress toward gender equality, highlighting regional differences (e.g., percentage of female legal professionals: 34.5% in the United States vs. 16.7% in the Middle East and North Africa). Users can explore the relationship between women's formal education and participation in the workforce, politics, and health. It also shows that women's attitudes are improving, with girls outnumbering boys in many regions. This data is invaluable for policymakers to justify equality efforts and for researchers to analyze the significant funding and structural factors affecting women.

But the most serious error is the gender classification system. Based on birth sex, this system uses a binary framework of male and female, ignoring transgender, non-binary women, and other gender-diverse people. Collecting data on these identities is challenging, especially in criminalized contexts, leading to significant underreporting.

Furthermore, this data lacks intersectionality. While it includes a gender breakdown, it does not reflect the inequalities of race, women, migrants, and people with physical limitations. This negatively impacts our analysis of the specific barriers women face and potentially perpetuates unequal conditions that favor some fortunate women.

This data does not include unpaid care work for children and family members, which has a significant negative impact on women's economic status and political participation. The data do not take into account traditional metrics like GDP or labor force participation rates. This analysis cannot calculate the value of care work or show how it affects paid work or political activity. Research shows that unpaid care work is a significant structural variable that limits women's ability to run for office.

This data also under-represents non-standard employment, which is essential in low-income countries. Women in non-standard employment and domestic work are often underrepresented, leaving gaps in the regular workforce and distorting women's economic status.

Furthermore, this analysis lacks high-quality data on women's lived experiences and social structures. Numerical data on political participation cannot account for women's underrepresentation due to factors like hatred, violence, partisan bias, and cultural norms, limiting our understanding of how inequality persists.

Data generation, quality, & information gaps

Governments (national and household surveys) and world groups use standard tools for data collection. Data collection includes interviews and discussions with census bureaus and provides metadata to document sources and methods. However, data quality and availability vary widely, resulting in "data gaps," particularly in low- and middle-income countries. Many census-sponsoring countries have weak civil registration and vital statistics systems, hindering understanding. When up-to-date data are unavailable, model estimates (projections) must be used. While standardization aids comparison, it can overlook contextual aspects of non-equivalent data and regional variations in meaning (e.g., "jobs").

Key data sources include census bureaus, demographic and household surveys (e.g., DHS), administrative records (e.g., school, health, and work), and world groups (political data) such as the International Population and People's Assembly (IPU). Major donors supporting gender data collection include Canada, Sweden, and the United Kingdom. The World Bank (Gender Group and DECDC) has a robust gender statistics project (SGS project) to support low-income countries. Globally, gender data is underfunded. Building a core system would cost approximately $500 million per year, nearly double current funding. Less than 0.5% of the COVID-19 response budget is dedicated to gender-related data.

Several information types are excluded by system type. The lack of data on violence against women (e.g., sexual violence, domestic violence, and political violence) in the data pile poses a major obstacle. Social norms, culture, attitudes, and gender views are not direct indicators (although external data piles such as the World Values ​​Survey provide indirect indicators). Information on construction factors that affect participation, such as childcare and access to public transportation, is also lacking. Data on discrimination in hiring, promotion, and credit is also lacking, as is information on women's political networks and education. Furthermore, mirror quality is lacking. Gender quotas in policies may be documented, but their effectiveness and implementation are not.

By focusing on countable indicators such as court appearances and reading comprehension rates, statistical analysis pushes the boundaries of other knowledge. This "counting bossification" conceals aspects essential to understanding inequality, such as hatred, emotional dynamics, and internal norms. If this were the only source of information, we might understand some aspects of women's political representation, but not the mechanisms behind them (party choice, voting trends, violence, caregiving barriers, etc.).

Data piles focused on country-level totals also conceal intersectional inequality. Treating "women" as a single box obscures the possibility that policies may benefit some women while negatively impacting women of certain racial, ethnic, or religious groups. Research has shown that gender quotas may even reduce, rather than increase, representation for minority women, a dynamic that the World Bank's standard binary analysis fails to capture. This effective method prioritizes gender-based analysis over intersectional analysis and positions this approach as a boss database.

Methodology & visualization selection

We selected visualizations based on clarity and accuracy. Some charts in our analysis were excluded because they lacked clear labeling, contained empty data, or mixed incomparable scales. Our principle: only use graphics that directly support the argument and are interpretable by readers. To understand why the US lags in representation despite comparable female educational attainment, we examined the direct comparison of enrollment and graduation trends between the United States and Canada.

Reflecting on data as argument

Data is not neutral. How we perceive "school," "appearance," and "winning" reflects our values ​​and choices. By being transparent about the data, limitations, and exclusions, we aim to demonstrate that this is an evidence-based fight, and that the truth does not reside in numbers. Our fight is that America is an outsider, and change is possible. The data bears this out. But the data also has silences, and human interpretation of these silences is part of humanity's goal to bring out.

    Annotation: The CEPR report argues that women drop out of STEM jobs at higher rates than men after graduation, which is changing the distribution of long-term employment. The report uses longitudinal cohort data and labor market records to document job transitions from STEM jobs to education, healthcare, and other fields that receive less public attention. This trend is important because different fields change public profiles and create a bait-and-switch web for political fundraising and bribes. For our paper, this report illustrates downstream mechanisms: even when women perform well, job selection can weaken their pipeline to public office by reducing the visibility of party gatekeepers and donors.

This research is important in translating research findings on the barrier into effective, proven policy answers. For this project, this report offers evidence-based interventions that can be proposed to translate increased women's school victories into measurable gains in political presence.
